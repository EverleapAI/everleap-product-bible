# Skills

## Purpose

The Skills page helps users understand **what they naturally do well and what capabilities they are developing**.

Unlike the Strengths page, which focuses on *how* a person approaches the world, the Skills page focuses on the abilities they repeatedly demonstrate and enjoy using.

This page is primarily informed by the **What Color Is Your Parachute? (WCIYP)** science model.

Its purpose is not to identify professional qualifications.

Its purpose is to recognize transferable skills that can be applied across school, careers, relationships, leadership, and life.

---

# The Question This Page Answers

Every page in Everleap answers one question.

The Skills page answers:

> **"What capabilities do I consistently demonstrate and enjoy using?"**

Everything on this page should help answer that question.

Nothing else.

---

# Philosophy

Skills are developed.

Some are discovered naturally.

Others are learned through experience.

Many become invisible because they feel effortless.

The goal of this page is to help users recognize capabilities they already possess and identify opportunities to continue developing them.

Everleap believes that understanding transferable skills gives people confidence far beyond choosing a career.

Skills travel with the person throughout life.

---

# Scientific Foundation

This page is primarily informed by **What Color Is Your Parachute?**

Rather than asking:

> "What job fits this person?"

Parachute asks:

> "What kinds of work does this person naturally enjoy doing?"

The focus is on transferable capabilities rather than occupations.

The science looks for recurring evidence about:

- preferred activities
- favorite problems
- natural contributions
- working style
- environments
- tools
- people
- recurring behaviors

The result is not a job recommendation.

It is a clearer understanding of how the user naturally creates value.

---

# What This Page Contains

The Skills page should help users recognize:

- transferable skills
- developing capabilities
- preferred activities
- favorite types of problems
- preferred work styles
- preferred collaboration styles
- recurring patterns of competence

The page should explain why these skills appear important.

---

# Transferable Skills

Transferable skills matter because they can be used almost anywhere.

Examples include:

- organizing
- teaching
- designing
- analyzing
- building
- writing
- solving problems
- communicating
- leading
- mentoring
- researching
- creating
- persuading
- planning

Everleap should emphasize these broader capabilities rather than narrow technical skills.

---

# Skills Are Not Grades

Grades measure performance in specific environments.

Skills describe capabilities that can be used throughout life.

Someone may struggle in one class while still demonstrating extraordinary transferable skills.

Everleap should help users separate capability from circumstance.

---

# What This Page Should Never Do

The Skills page should never:

- rank users
- compare users
- recommend careers
- measure intelligence
- assign aptitude scores
- imply that skills cannot change

Skills continue developing throughout life.

---

# Evidence

Every skill should be supported by evidence.

Evidence may include:

- Story responses
- repeated themes
- completed actions
- science memo observations
- historical consistency

If evidence is weak, uncertainty should be acknowledged.

---

# Confidence

Confidence reflects evidence.

Not mastery.

Low confidence means:

"We need more evidence."

It never means:

"The user lacks this skill."

The product should clearly distinguish between uncertainty and absence.

---

# Relationship To Other Pages

Motivations answers:

**Why do I care?**

Strengths answers:

**How do I naturally approach the world?**

Skills answers:

**What capabilities do I consistently demonstrate?**

Together they create a more complete picture than any one page could provide.

---

# AI Responsibilities

The AI responsible for Skills should:

- identify transferable skills
- explain supporting evidence
- recognize recurring capabilities
- acknowledge uncertainty
- encourage continued development

It should never:

- assign aptitude scores
- recommend careers
- exaggerate competence
- invent evidence

Its responsibility is recognizing patterns.

---

# Writing Style

The page should feel:

- practical
- encouraging
- thoughtful
- evidence-based
- specific

It should help users recognize abilities they may already possess without overstating them.

---

# Success

The Skills page succeeds when users think:

"I didn't realize that was actually a skill."

"I've been using that more than I thought."

"I can see how that applies in many different situations."

The page should increase confidence through understanding rather than praise.

---

# Anti-Patterns

Avoid:

- lists of buzzwords
- résumé language
- career predictions
- generic compliments
- unsupported observations
- technical jargon

Skills should always feel connected to the user's own experiences.

---

# Product Rules

Every skill observation should answer:

1. What capability appears to be emerging?
2. What evidence supports it?
3. Where does this capability seem strongest?
4. How might the user continue developing it?

The purpose of the Skills page is not to identify what someone can do today.

It is to help them recognize the capabilities they can continue building throughout their lives.
