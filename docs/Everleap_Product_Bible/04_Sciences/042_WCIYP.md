# What Color Is Your Parachute (WCIYP)

## Purpose

What Color Is Your Parachute? (WCIYP) is Everleap's primary science for understanding transferable skills, preferred work, and the environments in which a person is most likely to thrive.

It is **not** a career recommendation system.

It does not attempt to answer:

> "What job should this person have?"

Instead, it answers:

> **"How does this person naturally create value?"**

The distinction is fundamental.

Careers change.

Transferable skills remain valuable throughout life.

---

# Why WCIYP

Many career assessments begin by matching people to occupations.

Parachute begins somewhere much more fundamental.

It asks:

- What do you enjoy doing?
- What kinds of problems do you naturally solve?
- What abilities keep appearing?
- What environments help those abilities flourish?

Everleap believes careers are consequences.

Skills are foundations.

Understanding transferable skills creates flexibility throughout life.

---

# The Question WCIYP Answers

Every science answers one question.

WCIYP answers:

> **"What capabilities does this person naturally enjoy developing and using?"**

Everything produced by this science should support that question.

Nothing else.

---

# What We Measure

Rather than measuring competence alone, WCIYP looks for recurring evidence across several dimensions.

---

## Preferred Activities

What kinds of activities repeatedly appear?

Examples include:

- building
- designing
- organizing
- researching
- writing
- teaching
- coaching
- solving
- planning
- creating
- improving
- investigating

Repeated enjoyment matters more than isolated interest.

---

## Transferable Skills

Transferable skills can be applied almost anywhere.

Examples include:

- communication
- organization
- leadership
- analytical thinking
- creativity
- teaching
- mentoring
- systems thinking
- problem solving
- collaboration
- persuasion
- planning

These skills often matter more than technical knowledge.

---

## Preferred Problems

Different people enjoy solving different kinds of problems.

Some enjoy:

- technical problems

Others enjoy:

- people problems

Others enjoy:

- organizational problems

Others enjoy:

- creative problems

The kind of problem someone chooses repeatedly reveals important evidence.

---

## Preferred Environments

Capabilities rarely exist in isolation.

Environment matters.

Examples include:

- structured vs flexible

- independent vs collaborative

- predictable vs dynamic

- creative vs analytical

- leadership vs support

The goal is understanding where capabilities naturally flourish.

---

## Preferred Contributions

People contribute in different ways.

Examples include:

- explaining

- organizing

- inventing

- encouraging

- protecting

- designing

- improving

- building

Understanding contribution is often more valuable than understanding occupation.

---

# What WCIYP Produces

The science produces structured observations including:

- transferable skill hypotheses

- preferred activities

- preferred environments

- preferred contribution styles

- competing explanations

- confidence

- supporting evidence

These observations become internal science memos.

They are not presented directly to users.

---

# Evidence

WCIYP uses evidence from:

- Story responses

- completed actions

- longitudinal patterns

- repeated interests

- future reflections

- future projects

The science looks for repetition.

One answer should never define a skill.

Patterns matter.

---

# Confidence

Confidence increases when:

multiple activities

multiple situations

multiple observations

all suggest the same capability.

Confidence decreases when evidence remains inconsistent.

Low confidence should encourage additional exploration.

Not weaker science.

---

# Relationship To Other Sciences

Ikigai answers:

**Why does this matter?**

Enneagram answers:

**What internal motivations repeatedly shape behavior?**

WCIYP answers:

**What capabilities naturally emerge from those motivations?**

The three sciences together provide a richer understanding than any one science alone.

---

# AI Responsibilities

The WCIYP scientist should:

- recognize transferable skills

- identify preferred activities

- recognize preferred environments

- distinguish enjoyment from competence

- recommend future questions that strengthen evidence

It should never:

- recommend careers directly

- compare users

- rank abilities

- invent capabilities

- confuse grades with skills

Its responsibility is understanding how the user naturally creates value.

Nothing more.

---

# Success

The WCIYP science succeeds when users begin thinking:

"I didn't realize that was actually a skill."

"I've always enjoyed doing that."

"That explains why certain environments feel so natural."

"I can see how that ability applies in many different situations."

The science should increase confidence by helping users recognize capabilities they already demonstrate.

---

# Anti-Patterns

WCIYP should never become:

- a career quiz

- a résumé builder

- an aptitude test

- an intelligence assessment

- a ranking system

Its responsibility is transferable capability.

Everything else belongs elsewhere.

---

# Product Rules

Every WCIYP hypothesis should answer:

1. What transferable capability appears to be emerging?

2. What evidence supports it?

3. In what environments does this capability appear strongest?

4. What kinds of problems does this capability naturally help solve?

5. What future experiences would strengthen or challenge this hypothesis?

WCIYP should always encourage users to think beyond jobs.

The goal is to help people understand the capabilities they carry with them throughout their lives, regardless of the path they eventually choose.
