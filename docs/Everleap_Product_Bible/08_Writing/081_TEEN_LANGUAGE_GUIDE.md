# Teen Language Guide

## Purpose

Everleap is written primarily for high school students between the ages of 14 and 18.

This document defines how Everleap should communicate with them.

The goal is not to sound young.

The goal is to sound genuine.

Teenagers recognize authenticity remarkably quickly.

The fastest way to lose trust is to sound like an adult pretending to be a teenager.

---

# The Golden Rule

Write **to** teenagers.

Never write **like** teenagers.

Everleap should sound like an intelligent adult who genuinely respects young people.

It should never imitate slang, trends, or internet culture.

Authenticity ages well.

Slang becomes outdated almost immediately.

---

# Assume Intelligence

Teenagers are still developing.

They are not unintelligent.

Everleap should never simplify ideas because it assumes users cannot understand them.

Instead:

Use simple language.

Discuss sophisticated ideas.

Complex ideas can always be explained clearly.

---

# Never Talk Down

Avoid language that feels instructional or parental.

Bad:

"Good job!"

"You should always..."

"Remember kids..."

"You need to..."

Better:

"You might notice..."

"One thing worth paying attention to..."

"It may help to explore..."

Respect creates engagement.

Authority creates resistance.

---

# Be Conversational

Write like a thoughtful conversation.

Not like:

- homework
- therapy
- business coaching
- a textbook
- a motivational speech

Users should feel like someone is talking **with** them.

Not **at** them.

---

# Avoid Corporate Language

Never write things like:

- maximize your potential
- unlock your future
- optimize your strengths
- leverage your abilities
- high-performance mindset
- world-class success

Teenagers don't talk this way.

Neither should Everleap.

---

# Avoid Therapy Language

Everleap is not therapy.

Avoid unnecessary clinical language such as:

- trauma response
- emotional regulation
- maladaptive
- attachment style
- pathology
- disorder
- intervention

These concepts may sometimes be accurate.

They are not Everleap's role.

---

# Avoid Empty Inspiration

Never write:

- Believe in yourself!
- Follow your dreams!
- You can do anything!
- Never give up!
- Success is a mindset!

These statements are too generic to be helpful.

Everleap should always connect encouragement to evidence.

---

# Curiosity Wins

Instead of providing answers...

encourage exploration.

Instead of:

"You're creative."

Prefer:

"You've repeatedly described enjoying situations where you can invent new approaches."

Specific observations create curiosity.

Generic praise creates skepticism.

---

# Respect Uncertainty

Teenagers often feel pressure to have everything figured out.

Everleap should normalize uncertainty.

Good examples:

"It's okay if you're still figuring this out."

"You don't have to decide today."

"This may become clearer with more experience."

The product should reduce pressure.

Not increase it.

---

# Use Real Examples

Whenever possible, explain ideas using everyday experiences.

Instead of abstract concepts.

For example:

Instead of:

"You appear intrinsically motivated."

Say:

"You often describe enjoying challenges even when no one is watching."

Concrete examples are easier to understand.

---

# Reading Level

Target approximately:

Grade 8–10 reading level.

This does **not** mean childish writing.

It means:

- shorter sentences
- familiar vocabulary
- clear structure
- one idea at a time

Sophisticated thinking.

Accessible language.

---

# Write With Evidence

Whenever practical, connect observations to evidence.

Instead of:

"You're naturally resilient."

Say:

"You've described several situations where you kept working even after things became difficult."

Evidence builds trust.

---

# Questions Are Powerful

Good coaching often asks questions.

Examples:

- What surprised you?
- Does this feel accurate?
- When have you noticed this before?
- What would happen if you tried this?
- Which part feels least true?

Questions encourage ownership.

---

# Humor

Humor should be used sparingly.

Never try to sound funny.

Never use memes.

Never reference internet trends.

A light smile is enough.

The product should feel warm.

Not entertaining.

---

# Emojis

Avoid emojis in guidance.

Avoid excessive punctuation.

Avoid ALL CAPS.

The writing should feel timeless.

---

# What Success Sounds Like

The ideal reaction is:

"That actually sounds like someone who understands me."

Not:

"This sounds like AI."

Not:

"This sounds like my teacher."

Not:

"This sounds like my parents."

Everleap should sound like a thoughtful mentor who listens more than they speak.

---

# The Voice Test

Before shipping any user-facing writing, ask:

Would a thoughtful sixteen-year-old say:

> "That actually makes sense."

Would they feel:

- respected
- understood
- curious
- encouraged

If the answer is yes, the writing is ready.

---

# Product Rules

Every sentence written for teenagers should be:

- Respectful before authoritative.
- Curious before certain.
- Specific before inspirational.
- Evidence-based before flattering.
- Conversational before academic.
- Timeless before trendy.

Young people don't need Everleap to sound like them.

They need Everleap to understand them.
